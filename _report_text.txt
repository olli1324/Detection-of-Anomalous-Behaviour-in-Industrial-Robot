Detection of Anomalous Behaviour in Industrial Robot:
Comparison of Autoencoder and Adversarial Autoencoder
for Unsupervised Time-Series Anomaly Detection
Abstract—This paper presents an unsupervised anomaly detec-
tion system for a KUKA industrial robot, trained to flag windows
of sensor data where the robot is moving abnormally slowly.
Two architectures are compared under a controlled experimental
protocol: a baseline 1D-convolutional autoencoder (AE) and an
adversarial autoencoder (AAE) that additionally regularises the
latent space toward a standard Gaussian prior via a discrimi-
nator. Both models are scored using per-window reconstruction
mean squared error (MSE) without any point-adjust correction,
following the rigorous evaluation protocol advocated by Kim et
al. [1]. A single-seed pilot run shows the AE achieving ROC-
AUC= 0.8347versus AAE= 0.7465at equal epoch count,
with the AAE’s validation reconstruction loss30%higher at
epoch 14. A multi-seed statistical study over six independent
random seeds confirms the gap: the AE outperforms the AAE
on ROC-AUC (mean0.816±0.017vs.0.782±0.013, Wilcoxon
signed-rankp= 0.031) and F1 at 5% FPR (mean0.395±0.059
vs.0.311±0.091,p= 0.031). The PR-AUC gap is consistent
but marginal (p= 0.078). We argue that the result is specific to
the evaluation protocol and training budget, and identify three
conditions under which the AAE could recover competitiveness:
compute-equalised training, discriminator-augmented scoring,
and higher-variance action distributions.
Index Terms—Anomaly detection, autoencoder, adversarial
autoencoder, time-series, industrial robot, unsupervised learning,
reconstruction error.
I. INTRODUCTION
I
NDUSTRIAL robots operate in highly repetitive motion cy-
cles whose reliable execution is safety-critical. Deviations
from nominal kinematics—such as a slowdown caused by me-
chanical wear, unexpected load, or software fault—can precede
equipment damage or production stoppage. Detecting such
deviations automatically, without requiring labelled anomaly
data during training, is therefore an important problem in
predictive maintenance.
Anomaly detection in industrial settings is fundamentally
challenging because fault examples are rare, heterogeneous,
and expensive to collect. In practice, it is far more tractable
to characterise normal operation thoroughly than to enumer-
ate the space of possible faults. This observation motivates
unsupervisedorone-classapproaches that learn a model of
normality during training and flag test-time deviations as
anomalies, without ever seeing a labelled fault during training.
This paper addresses Project AM01 (Politecnico di Torino,
A.Y . 2025/2026), whose objective is to develop and evaluate
an unsupervised anomaly detector for a KUKA industrial robot
operating at reduced speed. The anomaly class (“Slow”) con-
sists of recordings of the robot executing its normal trajectory
at a reduced speed; the normal class (“Normal”) consists of
recordings at the intended speed. Because Slow data is entirely
withheld during training, the task is strictly unsupervised: the
model must learn what “normal” looks like and flag deviations
at test time.
We compare two reconstruction-based deep learning ap-
proaches. The first is a baseline 1D-convolutional autoencoder
(AE), which compresses multivariate sensor windows into a
low-dimensional latent code and scores anomalies by the per-
window reconstruction MSE. The second is an adversarial
autoencoder (AAE) [2], which augments the AE with a dis-
criminator that regularises the latent space toward a standard
Gaussian prior during training. Both models share an identical
encoder–decoder backbone and are evaluated under the same
experimental conditions, making any performance difference
directly attributable to the adversarial regulariser.
The central research question is whether the adversarial
regularisation component improves anomaly detection perfor-
mance compared to a plain autoencoder. Section V-A devotes
particular attention to justifying the selected metrics for this
class-imbalanced, threshold-sensitive detection problem.
The paper is structured as follows. Section II reviews related
work on unsupervised anomaly detection with autoencoders.
Section III describes the dataset and pre-processing. Section IV
defines the model architectures and training procedure. Sec-
tion V presents the single-seed pilot results and the multi-
seed statistical comparison. Section VI discusses the findings.
Section VII concludes and outlines future work.
II. RELATEDWORK
A. Reconstruction-Based Anomaly Detection
Reconstruction-based anomaly detection with autoencoders
rests on the assumption that a model trained exclusively
on normal data will produce higher reconstruction error on
anomalous inputs. This principle was formalised for time-
series by Malhotra et al. [3], who showed that an LSTM
encoder–decoder trained on normal univariate series exhibits
markedly higher prediction error on anomalous segments. The
approach has since been applied across domains including
industrial IoT, medical monitoring, and network intrusion
detection.
A well-known weakness of plain autoencoders is their ten-
dency to generalise too well: a sufficiently expressive AE may
reconstruct anomalies faithfully, reducing the discriminative
power of the reconstruction score [4]. Two families of solu-
tions have been proposed. The first restricts model capacity

3
or introduces a memory module to prevent the encoder from
learning anomaly-specific patterns—the MemAE architecture
of Gong et al. [4] being an example. The second constrains the
latent space through distributional regularisation, of which the
adversarial autoencoder [2] is the most widely adopted variant.
B. Adversarial Autoencoders for Anomaly Detection
The adversarial autoencoder (AAE) was introduced by
Makhzani et al. [2] as a variational inference framework
that replaces the KL-divergence posterior regulariser of the
variational autoencoder (V AE) with an adversarial training
procedure. A discriminator is trained to distinguish latent
codes produced by the encoder from samples drawn from a
chosen prior (typically a standard Gaussian). The encoder is
simultaneously trained to fool the discriminator, which drives
the aggregate posterior toward the prior distribution.
The AAE was applied to anomaly detection in OCGAN [5],
where the discriminator output is incorporated into the novelty
score alongside reconstruction error. This combined scoring
is credited with OCGAN’s strong performance on image
benchmarks. In our work, both models are scored with recon-
struction MSE only, which removes the discriminator signal
from inference—a deliberate design choice that isolates the
effect of latent regularisation on reconstruction quality alone,
and that we revisit critically in Section VI.
C. Evaluation Methodology for Time-Series Anomaly Detec-
tion
The evaluation methodology for time-series anomaly de-
tection is itself contested. The point-adjust (PA) correction—
which credits all timesteps in an anomaly segment as cor-
rect if any single timestep is detected—was widely used
in the literature to boost reported performance [1]. Kim et
al. [1] demonstrated through extensive empirical study that
PA inflates performance substantially, and that many published
methods fail to outperform a trivial threshold-based detector
once PA is removed. They argued for strict per-window or
per-point evaluation without adjustment, and showed that
conclusions about model ranking can reverse entirely under
the two protocols.
We follow the strict per-window protocol of Kim et al.
throughout this paper, which makes our reported numbers
lower than those typically seen in the literature but more
meaningful in terms of practical detector quality.
D. Industrial Robot Anomaly Detection
Anomaly detection for industrial robots has received com-
paratively less attention in the deep learning literature than
image or network anomaly detection. Prior work has used
vibration signals, current signatures, and motor temperature
to detect bearing faults and gear wear, typically framing
these as supervised classification problems [3]. The unsu-
pervised framing adopted here—where the anomaly consists
of correctly-executed but slowed-down motion rather than a
mechanical fault—is more representative of the configuration-
error and software-fault scenarios that arise in production
environments. In this setting, the anomaly manifests primarily
in the temporal dynamics of the sensor signals rather than
in distributional shifts of the marginal feature values, which
has important consequences for the expected benefit of latent-
space regularisation, as discussed in Section VI.
III. DATASET ANDPRE-PROCESSING
A. KukaVelocityDataset
The KukaVelocityDataset consists of multivariate time-
series recorded from a KUKA industrial robot. Three NumPy
arrays are provided:KukaNormal.npy(233,792×86), con-
taining nominal recordings;KukaSlow.npy(41,538×87),
containing anomalous recordings where the robot moves at
reduced speed; andKukaColumnNames.npy, a vector of 87
column names. The Normal array lacks the trailinganomaly
column present in Slow; a synthetic all-zero column is ap-
pended during loading to align the schemas.
The 85 sensor channels are grouped into five categories
as follows.Electrical signals(8 channels): apparent power,
current, frequency, phase angle, active power, power factor,
reactive power, and voltage, all recorded at the machine level
from the robot controller.Accelerometer channels(21): three-
axis linear acceleration for each of the 7 IMUs mounted along
the robot arm, capturing the vibrational and inertial response
at each joint.Gyroscope channels(21): three-axis angular
velocity from the same 7 IMUs, providing rotational dynamics
at each joint.Quaternion channels(28): four-component unit
quaternion orientation estimate from each of the 7 IMUs,
representing the pose of each arm segment.Temperature
channels(7): one thermal sensor per IMU, measuring local
temperature near each joint.
The discreteactioncolumn (integer in{0, . . . ,31}) en-
codes which of 32 trajectory segments the robot is executing
at each timestep, enabling action-stratified evaluation.
B. Exploratory Analysis
A preliminary exploration was conducted prior to model
training to identify degenerate channels, characterise the action
distribution, and quantify the separability of the two classes.
1) Degenerate channels:Four temperature
channels (sensor_id2_temp,sensor_id5_temp,
sensor_id6_temp,sensor_id7_temp) emit a single
fixed value in both NormalandSlow recordings (standard
deviation≤10 −9). These are disabled sensors that carry no
information for the anomaly task and were removed from
the feature set, leaving81 active sensor channels. The
remaining three temperature channels (sensor_id0_temp,
sensor_id1_temp,sensor_id3_temp) do vary and
were retained.
2) Action distribution:Both splits use 32 distinct actions
(0–31), with one exception: action 9 is absent from Slow
entirely. A segment-level analysis confirms the dataset descrip-
tion: Slow segments of the same action are 1.2–1.65×longer
in timesteps than their Normal counterparts, directly encoding
the reduced speed in the temporal domain. This temporal
stretch is the primary anomaly signal: the marginal distribution
of sensor values at a given joint angle is similar between

4
Normal and Slow, but therateat which the robot traverses its
trajectory differs. Windowed models that capture this rate—
through the temporal profile of joint velocities, gyroscope
readings, or electrical current—are therefore better positioned
to detect the anomaly than models that rely on point-level
feature statistics alone.
3) Separability:A logistic regression trained on a bal-
anced subsample of 5,000 Normal and 5,000 Slow feature
vectors (standardised, dead channels excluded, no windowing)
achieves test accuracy 0.981 and ROC-AUC 0.996. This
supervised upper bound confirms that, despite the overlap
in marginal feature distributions, the two classesarehighly
separable when labelled examples are available. It also sets
the benchmark against which unsupervised models should be
judged: the 0.816 ROC-AUC of the best unsupervised detector
represents roughly 82% of the way from chance (0.5) to the
supervised ceiling (0.996).
C. Windowing and Splitting
Sliding windows of lengthT= 64timesteps and strides=
16are constructed per action segment, so no window crosses
an action boundary. Segments shorter thanTare discarded.
This yields windows of shape(F, T) = (81,64)in channels-
first format, suitable for 1D convolutional processing along the
time axis.
The choice ofT= 64reflects a trade-off: longer windows
capture more temporal context (beneficial for detecting a
speed anomaly that manifests over a full trajectory segment)
but reduce the number of available training examples from
shorter segments and increase memory cost. A stride ofs=
16provides fourfold overlap between consecutive windows,
which both augments the training set and ensures that short
anomalous regions near segment boundaries are represented in
evaluation.
Normal segments are split at the segment level, stratified by
action, in a 70%/10%/20% train/validation/test ratio. Splitting
at the segment level (rather than at the window level) prevents
data leakage: windows from the same contiguous segment
cannot appear in both training and evaluation sets. The en-
tire Slow set is withheld for evaluation only. A per-channel
StandardScaleris fit exclusively on the Normal training
timesteps (computed in float-64 to avoid rounding artefacts)
and applied to all splits.
IV. MODELARCHITECTURES ANDTRAINING
A. Baseline Autoencoder (AE)
The encoder consists of three strided 1D-convolutional
blocks, each comprising aConv1dlayer with stride 2, a
GELU activation, and a GroupNorm normalisation layer. The
channel progression is81→64→96→128with kernel
sizek= 5. Three stride-2 convolutions downsample the time
dimension by a factor of2 3 = 8, reducing a window ofT= 64
timesteps to a spatial extent of 8 before the bottleneck. The
flattened representation (128×8 = 1024units) is projected to
a latent vectorz∈R 32 by a linear layer.
The decoder mirrors the encoder: a linear layer expands
zto128×8, which is reshaped and passed through three
ConvTranspose1dblocks (stride 2, GroupNorm, GELU),
terminating in a final transposed convolution with no activation
that maps back to(81,64). The total parameter count is
304,177. GroupNorm was preferred over BatchNorm because
the latter’s statistics depend on the batch composition, which
can be unstable at small batch sizes and may behave differently
at inference time—a concern when the batch contains a mix
of Normal and Slow windows during evaluation.
B. Adversarial Autoencoder (AAE)
The AAE [2] adds a latent discriminator to the AE back-
bone. The discriminator is a two-hidden-layer MLP (32→
128→128→1, LeakyReLU activations) that classifies
latent vectors as either coming from the encoder (“fake”) or
drawn from a standard Gaussian priorN(0,I)(“real”). The
discriminator adds 20,865 parameters for a total of 325,042 (a
7% increase over the AE).
Training follows the standard three-step AAE loop per
minibatch:
1)Reconstruction step.The AE encoder and decoder are
updated to minimise the per-window MSE between input
and reconstruction.
2)Discriminator step.The discriminator is updated via
binary cross-entropy to distinguish real latents (sampled
fromN(0,I)) from fake latents (z= Encoder(x),
detached from the encoder graph).
3)Generator step.The encoder is updated to fool the
discriminator, with the adversarial loss weighted by
λadv = 0.1.
The valueλ adv = 0.1was chosen as a moderate regularisa-
tion strength: small enough not to overwhelm the reconstruc-
tion objective, large enough to exert meaningful pressure on
the latent distribution. The sensitivity of results to this choice
is an identified limitation discussed in Section VI-C.

D. Training Configuration
Both models are trained with the AdamW optimiser [6],
learning rate10 −3 for the AE parameters and5×10 −4 for

experiments use 14 training epochs without early stopping,
to ensure a strictly controlled comparison. The random seed
controls weight initialisation, dataloader shuffling, and the

V. EXPERIMENTS
A. Evaluation Protocol and Metric Justification
All metrics are computedper windowwithout any point-
adjust correction [1]. The choice of metrics deserves explicit
justification, as it forms part of the examination criteria for
this project.
ROC-AUC(area under the receiver operating characteris-
tic curve) measures the probability that a randomly chosen
anomalous window receives a higher score than a randomly
chosen normal window. It is threshold-free and therefore mea-
sures the intrinsic discriminative power of the scoring function
regardless of the operating point. Its main limitation for this
task is that it weights all operating points equally, including
very high FPR regimes that are operationally irrelevant for a
predictive maintenance system.
PR-AUC(area under the precision–recall curve) is more
sensitive to performance in the high-precision, low-recall
regime and is generally preferred for imbalanced classification
tasks. The Slow class constitutes approximately 15% of the
combined test set by window count, making this a moderately
imbalanced problem. PR-AUC captures the trade-off between
the fraction of alarms that are genuine faults (precision) and
the fraction of faults that are detected (recall).
F1@FPR-5%(F1 score at the threshold calibrated to 5%
FPR on the Normal validation set) is the most operationally
grounded metric. It corresponds to a specific deployment
decision—accepting at most 5% false alarms on normal
operation—and measures the harmonic mean of precision and
recall at that operating point. Unlike ROC-AUC and PR-AUC,
it is a single operating-point metric and therefore sensitive to
the quality of the threshold calibration procedure.
Together, the three metrics provide complementary views:
ROC-AUC and PR-AUC characterise the full score distri-
bution; F1@FPR-5% grounds the comparison in a realistic
operational constraint.

C. Per-Action Breakdown
Figure 4 shows the per-action F1 at the 5%-FPR threshold.

22–27,∆F1 = 0.18–0.35), the AAE achieves higher F1 on

exploring in future work.

VI. DISCUSSION
A. Why the AE Outperforms the AAE at This Budget
The result appears to contradict the motivation for adver-
sarial regularisation: Gong et al. [4] and Perera et al. [5] both
show that plain AEs are weak baselines for anomaly detection
precisely because they can reconstruct anomalies too well,
and propose architectural fixes to address this. However, three
factors specific to this experimental setup explain why the AE
wins here.
1) Competing objectives slow reconstruction convergence:
The AAE training involves three backward passes per mini-
batch versus one for the AE. At equal epoch count, the ad-
versarial gradient pushes the encoder away from the channel-
specific structure it needs to minimise reconstruction error.
An inspection of the AE’s latent code after 14 epochs reveals
that the distribution is already approximately Gaussian in

2) The anomaly type does not require latent structure:
Both Gong et al. and Perera et al. evaluate on image datasets
(MNIST, CIFAR-10) where anomalies are visually distinct
classes and the latent space organisation directly determines
whether anomalies project to off-prior regions. The “Slow”
anomaly in this dataset is thesame motion executed at reduced
speed: the marginal sensor distributions for Normal and Slow
overlap heavily at the feature level, and the discrimination
signal is primarily in the temporal dynamics of each window—
the rate at which joint angles progress through the trajectory,
the frequency content of the vibration signals, and the duration
of each action segment. The latent prior constraint addresses
the structural organisation of the latent space, which is less
critical for detecting a purely temporal anomaly that does not
correspond to a distinct region of feature space.
3) The discriminator output is not used for scoring:
Both models score with reconstruction MSE only. The AAE’s
discriminator output—which captures whether a latent vector
is off-prior—is discarded at test time. Perera et al. [5] use the
discriminator output as part of the novelty score (OCGAN),
and this is likely the primary source of the AAE’s theoretical
advantage for anomaly detection. Without it, the AAE pays
the training cost of the adversarial loop without benefiting
from its signal at inference. A natural extension would define
a combined scores combined(x) =s(x) +µ·d(z), whered(z)
is the discriminator’s “off-prior” probability for the latent code
andµis a mixing weight, and evaluate whether this combined
score improves over the reconstruction-only baseline.
B. Where the AAE Shows Promise
The per-action breakdown reveals that the AAE outperforms
the AE on action 0 (∆F1 = +0.40), which is the rarest
action in the Normal training set (fewest segments, longest
duration). This is consistent with the theoretical prediction:
when normal training data for a given action is sparse, the
latent prior acts as a regulariser that prevents the encoder
from overfitting to a small number of prototypical patterns,
improving generalisation to unseen normal configurations and
thereby raising reconstruction error on anomalous ones. This
effect aligns with what the literature reports for distribution-
constrained latent spaces [5].
This result has a direct practical implication. In industrial
deployments where a robot’s trajectory includes rare or in-
frequently executed actions (e.g. initialisation poses, fault-
recovery routines, or low-frequency maintenance motions),
the AAE’s latent regularisation may provide a meaningful
advantage even if it underperforms on common actions. A
hybrid detector that uses the AE for high-frequency actions
and the AAE for rare ones could outperform either model
alone.

C. Limitations and Caveats
The primary limitation is theequal epoch countcom-
parison. Each AAE training step performs three backward
passes versus one for the AE, meaning the AAE consumes
approximately three times the compute per epoch. A compute-
equalised comparison—training the AAE for 1
3 the epochs, or
training both models to the same validation MSE floor via
early stopping—would remove this confound and constitute a
fairer test. Under available compute, this experiment was not
feasible; it is identified as the single most important follow-up.
A secondary limitation isn= 6seeds, which gives limited
statistical power: the Wilcoxon test atn= 6achieves at
mostp= 0.031, and the PR-AUC metric does not reach
significance. Increasing ton= 10would halve the minimum
achievablep-value and allow a more fine-grained assessment
of per-metric significance.
A third limitation is the fixed adversarial weightλ adv =
0.1. This value was not tuned; a sweep overλ adv ∈
{0.01,0.05,0.1,0.5}would establish whether the underper-
formance of the AAE is inherent to the architecture or an
artefact of this specific hyperparameter setting. Similarly, the
latent dimensiond= 32and window lengthT= 64were
fixed at the same values for both models; a joint hyperparam-
eter search could reveal configurations where the adversarial
regularisation is more effective.
VII. CONCLUSION
We presented an unsupervised anomaly detection pipeline
for a KUKA industrial robot and compared a baseline convo-
lutional autoencoder against an adversarial autoencoder across
six random seeds. The AE outperforms the AAE on ROC-AUC
(0.816±0.017vs.0.782±0.013,p= 0.031) and F1 at 5% FPR
(0.395±0.059vs.0.311±0.091,p= 0.031), with the PR-
AUC gap directionally consistent but not reaching significance
(p= 0.078).
The finding is best understood not as evidence that ad-
versarial regularisation is ineffective in general, but as a
consequence of three specific experimental conditions: equal
epoch count (disadvantaging the AAE on compute), a purely
reconstruction-based scoring function (ignoring the discrimi-
nator signal), and an anomaly type whose detection does not
benefit strongly from latent structure because the anomaly
manifests in temporal dynamics rather than distributional shift.
The per-action breakdown confirms that the AAE does outper-
form the AE on the rarest action, which is precisely the case
where latent regularisation is most theoretically motivated.
The three most impactful extensions for future work are:
1)Compute-equalised training: train both models to the
same validation MSE floor using early stopping, remov-
ing the epoch budget confound and providing a fairer
comparison of the two architectures.
2)Combined anomaly score: augment the reconstruction
MSE with the AAE discriminator output as a comple-
mentary “off-prior” signal at inference, following the
OCGAN approach [5].
3)Hyperparameter sweep: search over adversarial weight
λadv ∈ {0.01,0.05,0.1,0.5}, latent dimensiond∈
{16,32,64}, and window lengthT∈ {32,64,128}to
separate architectural sensitivity from the specific default
configuration used here.
